What's this about?

Excluding more complicated machine-learning algorithms, there's a whole bunch of more basic, statistical approaches to forecasting. Many of those involve using just the time-series itself rather than a large number of relevant features, and a relatively low computational cost.

(Moving) averages & exponential smoothing

The mean value of a time-series can often provide a very basic forecast, at least as long as the data is stationary and variance remains constant. The next best thing however when the time-series contains an underlying trend/seasonality can be a moving average: \[ f_{t+1}=1/k\cdot\Sigma_{t-k+1}^{t}y_t \] Where \(f_t\) is the forecast value and \(y_t\) the actual. A moving average will always tend to lag the time-series.
While as a forecast this might not be necessarily be the best that can be, it can capture trends - useful for building decomposition models.

More complex averaging methods are possible by adding weights to each value used. If these weights were set to increase exponentially towards the most recent value, then we have the exponential smoothing method. Instead of a more complex averaging equation we simply have (it is equivalent): \[ f_{t+1}=f_{t} + \alpha \cdot (y_t - f_t) \] Where \(\alpha\) is a parameter. Effectively each future forecast is adjusted by the latest known error.
