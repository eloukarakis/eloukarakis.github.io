Background info

There are three main approaches one could adopt to forecast prices and subsequently trade in the market.
The first is fundamental analysis, where one attempts to identify the intrinsic value of the investment through analysis of past company financials (or commodity demand).
The second is technical analysis, where one attempts to predict market dynamics based on past historic price patterns. Alternatively, through quantitative analysis - by applying more rigorous mathematical analysis on historical data, including assessing technical analysis approaches and their performance - it is possible to come up with trading methods with a certain degree of success. Finally, one may follow publicly available information and news to form an idea about how the market is expected to move.

Technical analysis rationale

The reasoning behind technical analysis is that there are inefficiencies which do not allow the market to perfectly discount available information about an asset's value. For example, there might be some insider activity that could start affecting an asset's price even before news break out. Furthermore, even when all news become available not all market participants will act at the same moment, while there might also be some momentum that results in the asset's price being much lower or higher than it is actually worth.

Technical analysis is based on the Dow theory which assumes that:
- The averages (market trend) will eventually discount everything
- There are three key trends in the market: a primary one which can last months to years; a secondary reaction which lasts weeks to months; and minor trends which last days to weeks.
- Primary trends have three phases: accumulation where informed participants buy more of the asset while others being bearish sell; trending where prices start to increase as confidence builds (or the opposite); distribution where informed participants sell off while others continue to buy (or the opposite) following the trend.
- A trend will persist until there is evidence to the contrary.
- Averages from different indices must give similar indications (a single indicator is not enough).
- Volumes must confirm the trend - i.e. they should be expanding in the direction of the existing trend.
