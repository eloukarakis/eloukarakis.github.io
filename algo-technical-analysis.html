Why go technical?

There are three main approaches one could adopt to forecast prices and subsequently trade in the market.
- The first is fundamental analysis, where one attempts to identify the intrinsic value of the investment through analysis of past company financials (or commodity demand). It is not always straightforward to interpret these numbers however, and a fair amount of educated guesswork is required to predict revenues over future years.
- The second is technical analysis, where one attempts to predict market dynamics based on past historic price patterns. One could take this a step further by applying more rigorous mathematical analysis on historical data, including assessing technical analysis approaches and their performance - that is through quantitative analysis. The key assumption however is that past price movements may be indicative of future prices movements.
- Finally, one may follow publicly available information and news to form an idea about how the market is expected to move. However, getting to the news first is critical to realising profits reliable, something that can be hard to do.

Technical analysis attempts to identify entry and exit points in the market and form an expectation on price movements, but with no concern as to why.
Analysis may be directly performed based on historic data time-series (e.g. OHLC, volume and other data) and to a certain degree in an automated manner.

The reasoning behind technical analysis is that there are inefficiencies which do not allow the market to perfectly discount available information about an asset's value. For example, there might be some insider activity that could start affecting an asset's price even before news break out. Furthermore, even when all news become available not all market participants will act at the same moment or in the same way.

The Dow theory

Technical analysis has its origins on the Dow theory. The latter assumes that:
- The averages (market trend) will eventually discount everything.
- There are three key trends in the market: a primary one which can last months to years; a secondary reaction which lasts weeks to months; and minor trends which last days to weeks.
- Primary trends have three phases: accumulation where informed participants buy more of the asset while others being bearish sell; trending where prices start to increase as confidence builds (or the opposite); distribution where informed participants sell off while others continue to buy (or the opposite) following the trend.
- A trend will persist until there is evidence to the contrary.
- Averages from different indices must give similar indications (a single indicator is not enough).
- Volumes must confirm the trend - i.e. they should be expanding in the direction of the existing trend.

Moving averages

First and foremost moving averages may be used as a price filter (filtering out short term volatility) that gives a clearer indication of price activity. The key relevant parameters are:
- length: shorter lengths will be more volatile and indicative of shorter term trends.
- calculation method: this may be as simple as averaging values equally weighted or more complicated, e.g. using an exponential moving average, or different weights when averaging (e.g. based on volumes).
- time displacement: the moving average may be displaced backwards or forwards to change the way it aligns with the data; this can affect how reliably it can be used as a support/resistance line.
- degree of smoothing: the moving average time-series could be averaged a second time to further remove volatility.

A moving average if appropriately tuned may serve as a support/resistance level, e.g. the 26 day average on the figure. The points where it crosses the prices may be considered as entry/exit points.
Alternatively, a point where a short term moving average crosses above a long term moving average may be considered as a reasonable entry (buy) point. The opposite applies for exit (sell) points. Commonly used values (apart from the 12/26 day shown on the example) are 50 and 100 day moving averages.
Taking the difference between the 12 and 26 day variables, effectively removes the underlying trend and results to an oscillator - negative on downward trends and positive on upward trends. This particular oscillator is named MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence) indicator. Taking the moving average of the oscillator gives a signal line which indicates entry and exit points when it crosses the MACD line. Note that these points come earlier than the ones indicated by the crossing of the averages.
