Derivatives & risk management

Derivatives are financial products whose value depends on the price of a specific asset. There is a variety of derivatives:
- forward contracts: agreement to purchase/sell at specific volume, date and price;
- future contracts: standardised form of forward contracts, facilitated through an exchange (which handles any credit default risks) with a clearly defined price;
- swaps: ...
- European put/call: right (but no obligation) to buy/sell a specific volume at specific date and price;
- American put/call: right (but no obligation) to buy/sell a specific volume at a specific price up to a specific date.

Pricing forward contracts

It is possible to do the following:
- buy a forward contract at t=0; this results in a cashflow at t=0: -f and at t=T: \(S_T-F\)
- short the asset; cashflow at t=0: \(S_0\)
- lend the value of asset; cashflow at t=0: \(-S_0\) and at t=T: \(S_0/d(0,T)\)
Where d(0,T) is the discount factor associated with the prevailing lending rate.
Based on the no-arbitrage principle the value F would be set equal to \(S_T\) therefore the value paid for the contract should be f=0. Consequently \(F=S_0/d(0,T)\)

Brownian motions

Brownian motion is a random process \(\{X_t:t \ge 0\}\) with drift \(\mu\) and volatility \(\sigma\) if for \(\Delta t>0\): \[X_{t+\Delta t}-X_t \sim N(\mu \cdot \Delta t, \sigma^2 \cdot \Delta t)\] If drift is 0, and volatility is 1 then we have a standard Brownian motion.
Geometric Brownian motion (GBM) is a random process if for \(t>0\) and \(W_t\) a standard Brownian motion: \( X_t=e^{(\mu-\sigma^2/2) \cdot t + \sigma \cdot W_t} \). Consequently: \[ X_{t+\Delta t} = X_t \cdot e^{(\mu-\sigma^2/2) \cdot \Delta t + \sigma \cdot (W_{t + \Delta t} - W_t)} \] GBM is often use to simulate stock prices. The model may be fitted to historic data by observing that: \[ ln(X_{t + \Delta t}) - ln(X_t) \sim N(\mu - 0.5 \cdot \sigma^2, \sigma \cdot \Delta t^{0.5}) \]
